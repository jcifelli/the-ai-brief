Oracle Fired 30,000 People With a 6 a.m. Email — to Pay for AI Data Centers

Sources: CNBC · The Next Web · Rolling Out

Oracle — one of the world’s biggest enterprise software companies — began what analysts believe is the largest mass layoff in its history yesterday morning. Estimates suggest between 20,000 and 30,000 employees are being cut, roughly 18% of the company’s global workforce of about 162,000.

Here’s the part people can’t stop talking about: employees across the U.S., India, Canada, and Mexico all received the same termination email at approximately 6 a.m. local time. No warning from HR. No call from a manager. Just an email from “Oracle Leadership” saying their role was eliminated and today was their last day. Their access to company systems was cut immediately.

“Today is your last day.” — The 6 a.m. email from “Oracle Leadership” sent simultaneously to thousands of employees across four countries

So where’s the money going? Straight into AI. Oracle has committed to $156 billion in capital spending on AI data centers — the giant facilities packed with specialized chips that train and run AI systems. According to investment bank TD Cowen, the layoffs will free up between $8 billion and $10 billion in cash flow to help fund that buildout. Oracle disclosed a $2.1 billion restructuring plan in its most recent SEC filing, with nearly $1 billion already spent.

Wall Street’s reaction? Analysts say the cuts will actually help Oracle’s stock by boosting profit margins. The human cost is enormous, but the financial logic is clear: Oracle is betting its future on AI infrastructure, and it’s paying for that bet with human jobs.

This is becoming a pattern in enterprise tech. Companies are cutting traditional roles while pouring money into AI. The question is whether the AI infrastructure they’re building will eventually create new jobs to replace the ones being eliminated — or whether this is a permanent shift toward fewer workers.

Perplexity AI Was Secretly Sending Your Conversations to Meta and Google

Bloomberg · Benzinga · The Decoder

A class-action lawsuit filed in San Francisco federal court accuses Perplexity AI — the AI-powered search engine that has positioned itself as a privacy-friendly alternative to Google — of secretly embedding tracking software in its code that sends your conversations to Meta (Facebook’s parent company) and Google.

According to the complaint, as soon as you log into Perplexity’s home page, trackers are downloaded onto your device, giving Meta and Google full access to your conversations with the AI search engine. This happens even when users turn on Perplexity’s “Incognito” mode, which is supposed to keep searches private.

The plaintiff, a Utah man identified as John Doe, said he shared personal information about his taxes, investments, and family finances with the AI chatbot, believing those conversations were private. The lawsuit calls the tracking “undetectable” and says it violated California privacy laws. Perplexity said it hasn’t been served the lawsuit and can’t verify the claims. Meta pointed to its rules saying advertisers shouldn’t send it sensitive data.

Trump Is Threatening to Pull Broadband Funding From States That Regulate AI — and It’s Working

Louisiana Illuminator · NPR · Broadband Breakfast

President Trump issued an executive order directing the government to withhold federal broadband expansion money — up to $800 million in some states — from any state that passes AI regulations his administration considers “burdensome.” He also directed the Department of Justice to challenge state AI laws in court.

It’s already having an effect. In Louisiana, at least seven out of nearly 20 proposed AI bills have been quietly shelved in the past week. Legislators from both parties confirmed they’re pulling their bills. The scrapped proposals would have: limited AI in health insurance decisions, required consent before AI records doctor-patient conversations, mandated disclosure when AI is used in hiring, banned AI from recreating someone’s identity without permission, and restricted “mental health chatbots.”

But not everyone is backing down. A handful of bipartisan proposals remain alive, including measures requiring disclosure of AI use and restricting AI in political advertising. The situation highlights a growing tension: Congress is deadlocked on AI regulation, states are trying to fill the gap, and the White House is using federal funding as leverage to stop them.

Whether you think AI regulation is a good idea or not, the practical result is that companies will have fewer legal obligations to tell you when AI is being used on you, and fewer restrictions on how they use it. Fewer state regulations means less compliance complexity for AI products you build — no patchwork of state laws to navigate. But it also means less legal clarity about responsible AI practices. On the Radar

A Federal Judge Just Called Trump’s Anthropic Ban “Orwellian”

U.S. District Judge Rita Lin blocked the Trump administration from banning Anthropic’s AI tools across all federal agencies. The dispute started when Anthropic pushed back against allowing the military to use its AI model Claude for domestic surveillance or fully autonomous weapons. In response, Trump ordered every federal agency to “immediately cease” using Anthropic’s technology and the Pentagon labeled the company a “supply chain risk.” Judge Lin called these actions “Orwellian” and ruled they constituted illegal retaliation against Anthropic’s First Amendment-protected speech. The injunction takes effect in seven days.

Eli Lilly Just Built Pharma’s Most Powerful AI Supercomputer

Eli Lilly inaugurated “LillyPod” — the pharmaceutical industry’s most powerful AI supercomputer. Built on Nvidia’s latest hardware, it packs over 1,000 of Nvidia’s Blackwell Ultra GPUs, delivering more than 9,000 petaflops of computing power (a petaflop is one quadrillion calculations per second). Lilly plans to use it to accelerate drug discovery — simulating how molecules interact, predicting which drug candidates will work, and shortening the years-long process of developing new treatments. It’s the clearest sign yet that AI in healthcare is moving beyond chatbots and into the core work of developing medicine.

Apple Is Rebuilding Siri With Google’s AI

Apple has officially confirmed that its reimagined Siri will be powered in part by Google’s Gemini AI model — a system with 1.2 trillion parameters (the internal settings that determine how an AI processes language). The new Siri will be “context-aware,” meaning it can see what’s on your screen and work seamlessly across apps instead of only responding to isolated voice commands. It’s the biggest admission yet that Apple’s in-house AI couldn’t keep up, and an unusual move for a company that famously prefers to build everything itself.
