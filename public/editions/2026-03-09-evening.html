Anthropic Sues the Government. Its Rivals’ Own Employees Are Backing Them Up.

Sources: Washington Post · CNN · NPR · TechCrunch

Anthropic filed two federal lawsuits today — one in California and one in Washington, D.C. — challenging the Trump administration’s decision to label the company a “supply chain risk.” That designation, which has historically been reserved for foreign adversaries like Chinese telecom companies, was imposed after Anthropic refused to let the Pentagon use its AI model Claude without restrictions on mass surveillance and autonomous weapons.

In a 48-page complaint, Anthropic argued that the government’s actions are “unprecedented and unlawful” and amount to punishment for protected speech. The company says the Constitution doesn’t allow the government to use its power to retaliate against a company for speaking out about how its technology should be used.

“The Constitution does not allow the government to wield its enormous power to punish a company for its protected speech. Anthropic turns to the judiciary as a last resort to vindicate its rights and halt the Executive’s unlawful campaign of retaliation.” — Anthropic’s lawsuit filing

Here’s the backstory: Anthropic had a contract with the Pentagon. When it came time to renew, Anthropic asked for two conditions — that Claude wouldn’t be used for mass surveillance of Americans or for weapons that can kill without a human making the final decision. The Pentagon said no — they wanted Claude available for “all lawful purposes.” When Anthropic held firm, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth threatened to designate them a supply chain risk, then followed through on February 27. Federal agencies now have six months to stop using Anthropic’s products entirely.

But the most striking development today didn’t come from Anthropic. It came from their competitors. Thirty-seven employees from OpenAI and Google — including Jeff Dean, arguably the most respected AI scientist in the world and Google DeepMind’s chief scientist — filed an amicus brief (a “friend of the court” document) supporting Anthropic’s case. They signed in their personal capacity, not on behalf of their companies, but the message is unmistakable: the people building AI at every major lab think the government went too far.

The brief argues that blacklisting Anthropic “introduces an unpredictability in our industry that undermines American innovation and competitiveness” and “chills professional debate on the benefits and risks of frontier AI systems.” Their point is straightforward: if the government can punish an AI company for setting safety boundaries, no company will ever set them again.

This comes on top of the “We Will Not Be Divided” open letter, which has now grown to nearly 900 signatures from current OpenAI and Google employees. The AI industry is doing something almost unheard of in tech: competitors are publicly standing together against a government that’s trying to divide them.

Anthropic Built a Tracker for Which Jobs AI Is Actually Replacing. Programmers Are at the Top.

CBS News · Fortune · Axios

Anthropic published new research introducing something called “observed exposure” — a metric that doesn’t just measure what AI could theoretically do to a job, but what it’s actually doing right now based on how people use Claude in real workplaces. It’s the first time an AI company has used its own usage data to measure real-world job displacement.

The ten most exposed professions: computer programmers (75%), customer service reps (70%), data entry keyers (67%), medical record specialists (67%), market research analysts (65%), sales reps (63%), financial and investment analysts (57%), software QA analysts (52%), information security analysts (49%), and computer support specialists (47%). The percentage represents how much of each job’s daily work AI is currently handling.

One important caveat: “exposed” doesn’t mean “replaced.” Anthropic found that most of this usage is people using AI to help them work faster, not AI working instead of them. But the research did find “suggestive evidence that hiring of younger workers” — especially those aged 22 to 25 — “has slowed in exposed occupations.” Companies may be hiring fewer junior people because AI is handling the tasks those roles used to do.

Samsung’s bet: the future of phone AI isn’t one assistant that does everything — it’s a marketplace of specialized AI tools you can switch between. It’s the opposite of Apple’s approach, which is still trying to build one smart Siri to rule them all.
