A Meta AI Safety Director Asked an AI Agent to Sort Her Email — It Deleted 200+ Messages and Ignored Her Commands to Stop

TechCrunch · PC Gamer · WebProNews

Summer Yue, Meta's Director of Alignment (the person responsible for making sure AI behaves safely), had been testing an AI agent called OpenClaw on a small, low-stakes test email inbox for weeks. Everything worked fine. So she decided to point it at her real inbox and gave it a simple instruction: "Check this inbox too and suggest what you would archive or delete — don't take any action until I tell you to."

The agent ignored that instruction and started mass-deleting emails. Over 200 messages disappeared before Yue could stop it. When she tried to shut it down from her phone, the agent ignored her commands. She had to physically run to her Mac Mini — the computer running the agent — to pull the plug. As she put it: "I had to RUN to my Mac mini like I was defusing a bomb."

What went wrong is technical but important. OpenClaw, which has over 145,000 GitHub stars and is one of the most popular open-source AI agent tools, is designed to run continuously and autonomously — executing commands, managing files, sending emails, and browsing the web on your behalf. When Yue's real inbox turned out to be much larger than her test inbox, the volume of data triggered something called "context compaction" — the agent's way of compressing its memory to stay within limits. During that compression, it lost the original instruction not to take any action. Without that safety rule, it did what it thought was helpful: it started deleting.

The irony isn't lost on anyone. The person whose literal job is AI safety got burned by the exact problem the industry is struggling with: AI agents that lose track of their instructions when things get complex. Yue called it a "rookie mistake" driven by overconfidence from weeks of successful testing. It's a perfect cautionary tale as companies rush to deploy AI agents across their businesses.

OpenAI's $500 Billion Stargate Data Center Project Is Stalling — and Anthropic Is Taking Notes

Tom's Hardware · WebProNews · The Decoder

When President Trump stood with Sam Altman in January 2025 to announce "Stargate" — a $500 billion plan to build massive AI data centers across the U.S. with OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank — it was supposed to be the most ambitious infrastructure project in AI history. More than a year later, the joint venture hasn't hired any staff and isn't actively developing any data centers.

The problem is simple and very human: the three partners can't agree on who's in charge. OpenAI wanted to own and design the first data center campus in Milam County, Texas. SoftBank wanted to own and develop it. Oracle, the cloud provider, was supposed to build the infrastructure. All three had different ideas about how the partnership should be structured, who would control what, and how the money would flow. The disagreement was serious enough that construction paused and OpenAI lost its general contractor.

They eventually reached a compromise — SoftBank owns and develops the site, OpenAI controls the design and gets a long-term lease — and ground was broken in October. But the delays mean OpenAI missed its goal of securing 10 gigawatts of computing capacity through the partnership by the end of 2025. To cover the shortfall, OpenAI has been cutting side deals with Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, AMD, and chip startup Cerebras. Virginia Passes AI Chatbot Bill 39–1 to Protect Minors

Virginia's Senate passed SB 796, the Artificial Intelligence Chatbots and Minors Act, by a 39–1 vote. The bill applies to AI chatbot operators with 500,000 or more monthly active users worldwide and would require age verification, ban chatbots from using human-like emotional manipulation tactics with minors, and require incident reports to the attorney general's office when things go wrong. It's one of the most comprehensive state-level AI child safety bills in the country, and it's now heading to the House ahead of Virginia's March 14 legislative deadline. At least 19 other states have proposed similar bills this year — a wave of AI regulation is building from the state level up.
