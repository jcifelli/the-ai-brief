A Chinese AI Tool Made a Fake Tom Cruise Fight Scene So Real That Hollywood Lawyered Up

Sources: CNN · TechCrunch · CNBC · Al Jazeera

Here's what happened: ByteDance — the Chinese company that owns TikTok — released a new AI video tool called Seedance 2.0. Within days, someone used it to generate a clip of Tom Cruise and Brad Pitt in a fistfight on a post-apocalyptic rooftop — verbally sparring about Jeffrey Epstein. That clip got 3.2 million views. It looked real. The characters moved like real people. And it took a two-line text prompt to create.

"My glass half empty view is that Hollywood is about to be revolutionized/decimated." — Rhett Reese, writer and producer of the Deadpool franchise

Hollywood's response was immediate and furious. Disney, Netflix, Paramount, Warner Bros, and Universal all sent ByteDance cease-and-desist letters. The Motion Picture Association accused the tool of "unauthorized use of U.S. copyrighted works on a massive scale." SAG-AFTRA — the actors' union — condemned "the unauthorized use of our members' voices and likenesses" and called it a violation of "law, ethics, industry standards and basic principles of consent."

Seedance 2.0 is currently available in China through ByteDance's Jimeng app, but it's about to be integrated into CapCut — the popular video editing app for TikTok users worldwide. It generates 15-second clips now, with longer clips coming. One Chinese tech blogger discovered the tool could generate realistic audio of his voice based solely on a photo of him. ByteDance rolled that feature back after the backlash.

The bigger picture is striking. Chinese open-source AI models went from near-zero usage in mid-2024 to about a third of all AI use by late 2025. Hollywood is trying to use copyright law to rein in these tools, but the legal systems that govern copyright in the U.S. have no enforcement power in China. Entertainment lawyer Jonathan Handel told Al Jazeera the situation marks "the beginning of a difficult road" for the film industry, predicting that "in several years, full-length movies that are AI-generated" will be a reality.

New York Wants to Force News Organizations to Label AI-Generated Content

Nieman Lab · NY Senate · City & State NY

New York State lawmakers introduced the NY FAIR News Act — a bill that would require any news organization operating in the state to put clear disclaimers on content "substantially composed, authored, or created" by AI. It would also require that a human editor review AI-generated material before publication, protect journalist sources from being accessed by AI systems, and prohibit using AI in ways that displace journalists or reduce their wages. The bill has support from the Writers Guild, SAG-AFTRA, the Directors Guild, and the NewsGuild of New York. It does not yet have support from broadcasters or publishers.

The bill's sponsors cite two concerns: that AI-generated news content can be false or misleading, and that it "plagiarizes" by drawing from original reporting without permission. If passed, New York would become the first state to regulate AI use in newsrooms specifically.
