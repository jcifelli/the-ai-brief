OpenAI’s Head of Robotics Walked Out Over the Pentagon Deal. Here’s Why That Matters.

Sources: TechCrunch · Fortune · Engadget · SF Standard

Yesterday, Caitlin Kalinowski — the executive who ran OpenAI’s robotics division — resigned in protest over the company’s new agreement with the Pentagon. She’d been at OpenAI since November 2024, having previously led the team building augmented reality glasses at Meta.

In her resignation post on X and LinkedIn, Kalinowski didn’t mince words: she supports AI playing a role in national security, but said the deal was rushed through without proper safeguards on two critical issues — surveillance of Americans without a judge’s approval, and weapons that can kill without a human making the final call.

“Surveillance of Americans without judicial oversight and lethal autonomy without human authorization are lines that deserved more deliberation than they got.” — Caitlin Kalinowski

In a follow-up post, she clarified that her concern wasn’t with working with the military at all — it was a governance problem. The announcement was rushed, the guardrails weren’t defined, and these were decisions too important to skip the deliberation on. “This was about principle, not people,” she wrote, while saying she still has deep respect for Sam Altman and the OpenAI team.

Here’s the context: OpenAI signed a deal to deploy its AI models on a classified Pentagon network. On paper, the agreement includes red lines — no domestic mass surveillance, no directing autonomous weapons. But critics, including the Electronic Frontier Foundation, say the language is full of wiggle room. The contract bans “intentional” use for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons, but doesn’t define what counts as “intentional” or how violations would be enforced. Even Altman himself admitted the deal “looked opportunistic and sloppy.”

Why this matters beyond OpenAI: Kalinowski is the first senior executive at a major AI company to resign specifically over a military contract. She’s not some random employee signing a petition — she ran a major division. Her departure comes on top of the nearly 900 workers across Google, OpenAI, and other companies who’ve signed open letters calling for limits on military AI work. The message from inside these companies is getting louder: the people building this technology are not comfortable with how fast it’s being handed to the military.

Steve Bannon and Ralph Nader Agree on Something: Humans Should Stay in Charge of AI

TechCrunch · NBC News · Pro-Human AI Declaration

Here’s something you don’t see every day: a political document signed by both Steve Bannon and Ralph Nader, Glenn Beck and Susan Rice, labor unions and religious organizations, all agreeing on the same thing. The “Pro-Human AI Declaration,” released Friday with more than 40 organizations and hundreds of individual signatories, lays out five principles for how AI should be governed.

The principles are: keep humans in charge of AI at all times; prevent any single company or government from concentrating too much AI power; protect the human experience from being replaced by synthetic content; preserve individual liberty; and hold AI companies legally accountable for harm their products cause.

The most muscular provision: an outright ban on developing superintelligence — AI that surpasses human intelligence across the board — until there’s scientific consensus it can be done safely and genuine democratic buy-in from the public. The declaration also calls for mandatory “kill switches” on powerful AI systems, pre-deployment safety testing (especially for chatbots and companion apps aimed at young people), and a ban on AI architectures that can self-replicate or autonomously improve themselves.

Polling cited in the declaration says 95% of Americans oppose an unregulated race to superintelligence. Whether Congress acts on any of this is another question entirely — but the bipartisan support makes it harder to dismiss as a fringe concern.
