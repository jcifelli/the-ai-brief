88 Countries Just Agreed on Rules for AI — Including the US, China, and Russia

Sources: Zee News · Business Today · ChinaTechNews · TIME

Here's what happened: India just pulled off something that seemed almost impossible in 2026 — it got 88 countries, including the United States, China, and Russia, to sign the same document about how AI should be developed and governed. The New Delhi Declaration on AI Impact was formally adopted this morning as the AI Impact Summit wrapped up its sixth and final day at Bharat Mandapam in New Delhi.

"The benefits of artificial intelligence must be equitably shared. AI's future cannot be left to the whims of a few billionaires." — UN Secretary-General António Guterres, addressing the summit

The declaration is built around seven pillars — India called them "chakras" — covering everything from building AI talent, to making AI accessible to poorer countries, to ensuring AI systems are trustworthy and energy-efficient. It's not legally binding, which means no country is forced to follow it. But getting the US, China, and Russia to put their names on the same AI framework is significant by itself — those three countries rarely agree on anything, let alone emerging technology policy.

The summit was massive. Over 500,000 people attended in person across the six days, more than 20 heads of state showed up, and tech companies made investment pledges worth over $250 billion. Sam Altman of OpenAI and Dario Amodei of Anthropic were both in attendance — though, as CNBC noted, they carefully avoided being photographed together. Indian conglomerate Adani pledged $100 billion for AI data centers powered by renewable energy by 2035. OpenAI, Nvidia, and Microsoft all announced India-specific partnerships.

The deeper story: India is positioning itself as a bridge between the wealthy nations building AI and the developing countries worried about being left behind. The declaration's emphasis on "AI for All" is a deliberate counterpoint to the AI race between the US and China, framing AI as something that should benefit everyone, not just the countries that can afford to build it. Whether the declaration changes anything in practice depends on what comes next — but getting the signatures was the hard part.

Hollywood Forced ByteDance to Pull Back Its Viral AI Video Tool After Deepfakes of Tom Cruise and Spider-Man Flooded the Internet

Deadline · CNN · Variety · CNBC

ByteDance — the company that owns TikTok — released a new AI video generator called Seedance 2.0 on February 12th, and things spiraled fast. Within hours, people were using it to create incredibly realistic fake videos of Tom Cruise fighting Brad Pitt on a rooftop, Donald Trump doing kung fu in a bamboo forest, and Kanye West dancing through a Chinese palace. It also generated videos featuring copyrighted characters like Spider-Man, Darth Vader, and Baby Yoda — which is when Hollywood's lawyers got involved.

Disney sent a cease-and-desist letter. Then Paramount. Then Netflix. Then Sony. The Motion Picture Association called it "unauthorized use of U.S. copyrighted works on a massive scale." Paramount accused ByteDance of "blatant infringement," specifically calling out AI-generated content featuring South Park, Star Trek, The Godfather, and Dora the Explorer. Spotify's Best Engineers Haven't Written a Line of Code in Two Months — AI Does It All Now

TechCrunch · AI Magazine · Fast Company

During Spotify's fourth-quarter earnings call, co-CEO Gustav Söderström dropped a bombshell: the company's most senior developers "have not written a single line of code since December." Instead, they use an internal system called Honk, built on top of Anthropic's Claude Code, to write software by giving instructions in plain English through Slack.

Here's how it works: a Spotify engineer on their morning commute can open Slack on their phone, tell Claude to fix a bug or build a new feature for the iOS app, and by the time they reach the office, a working version of the app is waiting for them to review and merge into production. The system shipped more than 50 new features throughout 2025, including AI-powered playlists and audiobook features. Spotify's other co-CEO, Alex Norström, called 2026 the "Year of Raising Ambition."

This isn't "developers using AI to help write code" — this is developers supervising AI that writes all the code. It's a fundamentally different job. The UN Wants a $3 Billion Global Fund So AI Doesn't Just Belong to Rich Countries

UN Secretary-General António Guterres used his address at the India AI summit to push for a $3 billion international fund to ensure poorer countries get access to AI technology. He argued that leaving AI development "to the whims of a few billionaires" would deepen global inequality. The proposal is part of a broader push to create an International AI Panel — a scientific advisory body, similar to what exists for climate change — to guide global AI governance. Whether wealthy nations and tech companies fund the proposal remains to be seen.
