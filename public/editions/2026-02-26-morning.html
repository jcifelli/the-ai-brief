Nvidia Just Posted $68 Billion in a Single Quarter — and Says Next Quarter Will Be Even Bigger

Sources: CNBC · Fortune · Nvidia Newsroom

Nvidia, the company that makes the specialized chips powering nearly every major AI system in the world, reported last night that it earned $68.1 billion in revenue in its most recent quarter — up 73% from the same period a year ago. That number alone would be impressive for any company's full-year revenue. Nvidia did it in three months.

But the number that really matters is the forecast. Nvidia told investors to expect $78 billion in revenue next quarter, which is $5 billion higher than what Wall Street's most optimistic analysts had predicted. That gap between what experts expected and what Nvidia is projecting tells you something important: the companies buying AI chips — think Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta — are spending even faster than the people whose job it is to track spending thought they would.

"Computing demand is growing exponentially — the agentic AI inflection point has arrived." — Jensen Huang, Nvidia CEO

For the full year, Nvidia pulled in $215.9 billion in total revenue, up 65% from the prior year. Its data center business — the division that sells AI chips to cloud providers and tech companies — accounted for over 91% of all revenue, at $62.3 billion for the quarter alone. A year ago, people were debating whether AI spending was a bubble. Vera Rubin promises to deliver 10 times more performance per watt than the current Blackwell chips — meaning AI companies could train and run the same models using a fraction of the energy and cost. A Viral "Doomsday Memo" Predicting AI-Driven Mass Unemployment by 2028 Is Rattling Markets — and Starting a Real Conversation

Euronews · Entrepreneur · Citrini Research

A fictional memo written as if from the year 2028, titled "The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis," has gone viral and spooked financial markets. Written by James Van Geelen of Citrini Research and AI entrepreneur Alap Shah, the paper imagines looking back at how AI-driven layoffs created a cascade of economic damage — starting with white-collar job cuts in 2026, escalating into a full recession by 2027, and ending with 10% unemployment and a new mortgage crisis by 2028.

The scenario goes like this: companies start replacing workers with AI agents, each individual company's decision is rational, but the collective result is catastrophic. Displaced office workers flood into lower-paying jobs and blue-collar trades, wages stagnate across the board, consumer spending drops, and the economy tips over. The authors call this "ghost GDP" — where economic output keeps growing on paper because AI is producing more, but real household income is falling because fewer people are earning a paycheck.

The memo is a thought experiment, not a prediction — the authors are clear about that. But its timing was perfect. It went viral the same week that Amazon, Pinterest, and Expedia all announced layoffs partly attributed to AI, and economists are publicly debating whether companies are genuinely replacing workers with AI or simply using AI as a convenient excuse to cut costs.
