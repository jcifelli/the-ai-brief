OpenAI’s Wild Tuesday: Sora Killed, Disney Gone, Safety Sidelined, and a Mysterious New Model

Sources: Tom’s Guide · Deadline · Variety · Slate · Techmeme

Tuesday was one of the most dramatic days in OpenAI’s history. In a single staff meeting, CEO Sam Altman announced the company would shut down Sora — its AI video generator that turns text descriptions into short videos — just six months after launching it with fanfare. The iOS app, the website, and the developer tools are all being taken offline.

The fallout was immediate. Disney, which had signed a deal just three months ago to invest $1 billion in OpenAI and license iconic characters like Mickey Mouse for use in Sora, pulled out entirely. No money had changed hands yet, but the partnership is dead. “The deal is not moving forward,” a Disney spokesperson confirmed to Deadline.

But that wasn’t all. The same day, The Information reported that Altman has handed off direct oversight of OpenAI’s safety and security teams to focus on fundraising, supply chains, and “building data centers at unprecedented scale.” And he revealed that OpenAI has finished initial development on a new AI model codenamed “Spud” — though he offered no details beyond a promise that it would “really accelerate the economy.”

“The compute resources and engineering attention the Sora app consumed are now redirected toward enterprise AI, agentic systems, and coding tools.” — OpenAI internal communication, per Tom’s Guide

The strategic picture is clear: OpenAI is making a hard pivot. Instead of flashy consumer products like video generation and AI-powered shopping (ChatGPT’s checkout feature also flopped, with Walmart abandoning a pilot last week), the company is going all-in on AI agents — programs that can complete complex tasks on their own — and tools for business customers. The company is also preparing for an IPO.

The safety question is the most sensitive part. Altman stepping away from safety oversight continues a pattern that has alarmed researchers — OpenAI’s original safety team dissolved in 2024, and multiple senior safety researchers have left the company. With an IPO on the horizon and billions in data center spending planned, the worry is that safety is being deprioritized at exactly the moment OpenAI is building its most powerful systems yet.

Google Found a Way to Make AI Use 6x Less Memory — and Memory Chip Stocks Are Tanking

CNBC · TechCrunch · VentureBeat

Google unveiled a new technique called TurboQuant that dramatically reduces how much computer memory AI models need to run. Here’s the plain English version: when an AI model processes your request, it stores calculations in a kind of scratchpad so it doesn’t have to redo them. That scratchpad eats up a lot of expensive memory chips. TurboQuant compresses that scratchpad to just 3 bits per value, down from the standard 16 — shrinking it by at least 6x without losing accuracy.

The result: AI models can run on Nvidia’s H100 chips up to 8x faster for certain tasks, and the cost of running AI could drop by 50% or more. TechCrunch is calling it the “Pied Piper moment” (a reference to the fictional compression company from Silicon Valley). The internet agrees.

Wall Street noticed immediately. Shares of SK Hynix and Samsung — the world’s two biggest memory chipmakers — fell 6% and 5% respectively on Thursday. If AI needs dramatically less memory to run, demand for memory chips could slow down, and billions of dollars in planned memory chip factories may need rethinking.

Arm Made Its First Chip Ever — and It’s Designed for the Age of AI Agents

CNBC · Tom’s Hardware · Arm Newsroom

For 35 years, Arm has been the company behind the chip designs in basically every smartphone on Earth — but it never made its own chips. It licensed its designs to other companies (Apple, Qualcomm, Samsung) and collected royalties. That just changed.

Arm unveiled the AGI CPU, its first in-house chip: a 136-core, 3-nanometer processor built specifically for running AI in data centers. The chip is designed for “inference” — that’s the industry term for when an AI model processes your question and generates an answer, as opposed to the training phase where the model learns. Meta is the launch partner, and OpenAI, Cerebras, Cloudflare, and SAP have also signed up.

Arm’s stock jumped 16% on the news — the biggest single-day gain in almost a year. The company expects its new chip business to generate $15 billion in revenue by 2031, up from $4 billion total in 2025. It’s a direct challenge to Intel and AMD in the data center market, and it signals that the demand for AI processing power is big enough to lure a 35-year-old licensing company into manufacturing.

Anthropic and the Pentagon May Be Close to a Deal — After Weeks of Very Public Fighting

Axios · CNBC · Al Jazeera

The drama between Anthropic (maker of Claude) and the Pentagon could be nearing a resolution. Axios reported this morning that the two sides are in private discussions, with Anthropic exploring a compromise on the language governing how the military can use Claude.

Here’s the backstory: Anthropic refused to let the Pentagon use Claude for autonomous weapons targeting or mass surveillance of Americans, citing safety concerns. The Trump administration retaliated by labeling Anthropic a “Supply-Chain Risk to National Security” — effectively blacklisting it from government contracts. Anthropic sued, and a federal judge called the Pentagon’s actions “troubling,” noting the government’s reasoning seemed like “a pretty low bar.”

Court filings revealed that the two sides were actually “very close” to a deal before the public fallout. A Pentagon negotiator had emailed Anthropic saying “we are very close here” — just five days before Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared the company a national security risk. A ruling from the judge could come any day.

What does it mean for me? This fight is about whether there should be limits on how the government uses AI. Claude Can Now Control Your Mac While You’re Away

Anthropic launched “computer use” in Claude Cowork and Claude Code, letting Claude physically control your Mac — opening apps, clicking buttons, typing text, and completing multi-step tasks on your behalf. It’s Mac-only for now, available to Pro and Max subscribers, and Claude asks permission before accessing new apps. This is the closest any AI has come to being an actual digital assistant that operates your computer like a human colleague would.

Three People Charged With Smuggling $2.5 Billion in AI Chips to China

The U.S. Department of Justice charged three people associated with AI server maker Super Micro Computer, including its co-founder, with helping smuggle at least $2.5 billion worth of American AI technology to China in violation of export laws. It’s one of the largest alleged export control violations in the AI era, and it underscores just how valuable — and how tightly controlled — advanced AI hardware has become.
