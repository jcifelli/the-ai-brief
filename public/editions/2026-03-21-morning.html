The White House Wants to Override Every State AI Law in America

Sources: NBC News · CNN · PBS · Roll Call · Fortune

The Trump administration released a sweeping national AI policy framework on Friday that amounts to the most aggressive federal power grab over AI regulation yet. The core message to Congress: pass one national standard for AI rules and block states from writing their own.

The framework has two big ideas. First, it calls for “preemption” of state AI laws — meaning federal rules would override the patchwork of state regulations that have been popping up across the country. States like Colorado, California, and New York have been writing their own AI rules around things like bias audits (independent checks to see if AI systems discriminate), transparency requirements, and liability for AI-caused harms. The White House wants Congress to replace all of that with a single, lighter set of national rules.

Second, the framework pushes hard to protect AI developers from lawsuits. It specifically says companies that build AI models shouldn’t be held legally responsible when someone else uses their product to cause harm. The document rails against “open-ended liability” and argues that too many lawsuits would slow down American AI innovation.

“Congress should preempt state AI laws that impose undue burdens to ensure a minimally burdensome national standard consistent with these recommendations, not fifty discordant ones.” — White House National AI Policy Framework

The framework also touches on children’s safety, intellectual property, free speech protections, data center permitting, and workforce development. On kids, it calls for giving parents better tools to manage their children’s AI interactions — but notably puts the burden on parents rather than on AI companies. On intellectual property, it tries to balance the rights of content creators with the AI industry’s need to train models on real-world content, though critics say it tilts heavily toward the industry.

The reaction was immediate and split. AI companies largely welcomed the lighter-touch approach. Consumer advocates and state attorneys general pushed back hard, arguing that preempting state laws would leave Americans with weaker protections. The White House says it will work with Congress to turn the framework into legislation, but many policy experts doubt anything will pass before the midterm elections in November.

The timing is notable. This framework arrives just two weeks after the administration blacklisted Anthropic for refusing Pentagon demands, and days after Nvidia’s GTC conference showed the AI industry is accelerating, not slowing down. The message is clear: the White House wants AI companies to build as fast as possible with as few legal obstacles as possible.

Supermicro’s Co-Founder Was Arrested for Allegedly Smuggling Billions in AI Chips to China

CNBC · Fortune · NBC News

Federal agents arrested Wally Liaw, who co-founded server giant Super Micro Computer in 1993, on charges that he helped smuggle $2.5 billion worth of AI servers containing banned Nvidia chips to China. Two alleged co-conspirators — Supermicro’s Taiwan general manager Steven Chang (now a fugitive) and a logistics fixer named Willy Sun — were also charged.

The scheme was elaborate: according to the indictment, the three men routed servers through a front company in Southeast Asia, which would repackage them and remove identifying labels — allegedly using hair dryers to peel off serial numbers — before shipping them to their real destination in China. About $510 million worth of servers with banned chips made it through.

Supermicro’s stock cratered 33% on Friday — its worst single day since 2018. The company itself wasn’t charged and says it cooperated with investigators. Each defendant faces up to 20 years in prison for violating export control laws. The case is the most dramatic example yet of what U.S. officials say is a widespread effort to get advanced AI hardware into China despite a ban that’s been in place since 2022.
