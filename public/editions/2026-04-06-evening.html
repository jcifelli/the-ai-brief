OpenAI Says America Needs Robot Taxes, a Wealth Fund, and a 32-Hour Workweek to Survive Superintelligent AI

Sources: OpenAI · Axios · Fortune · Bloomberg

OpenAI — the company that makes ChatGPT — released a 13-page document today titled “Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age.” It’s essentially a blueprint for how the U.S. government should prepare for AI that’s smarter than humans. And the proposals are surprisingly bold coming from the company that would be most affected by them.

The headline ideas: First, OpenAI is calling for new taxes on automated labor. As AI replaces jobs that humans used to do, the payroll taxes that fund Social Security and Medicare dry up. OpenAI wants to replace that lost revenue by taxing the companies that use AI to do the work instead. Second, they want the government to create a nationally managed public wealth fund — seeded in part by AI companies themselves — that would give every American a direct financial stake in AI-driven economic growth. Think of it as a national investment account where the gains from AI get shared, not just concentrated among tech company shareholders.

“AI superintelligence is so close, so mind-bending, so disruptive that America needs a new social contract — on the scale of the Progressive Era and the New Deal.” — Sam Altman to Axios

Third, OpenAI is proposing pilot programs for a 32-hour workweek at full pay, framing it as an “efficiency dividend” — the idea being that if AI makes workers significantly more productive, they should work fewer hours rather than just generate more profit for employers. The document also includes a section on “containment playbooks” for dangerous AI systems that become autonomous and can’t be easily shut down, proposing government coordination as the response.

Not everyone is buying it. Critics, including several AI policy researchers quoted by Fortune, say the proposals are deliberately vague and amount to “regulatory nihilism” — bold-sounding ideas designed to look like leadership while avoiding any specific regulation of OpenAI itself. The document says nothing about slowing down AI development, and some see it as a way for OpenAI to position itself as the responsible adult in the room while racing ahead at full speed.

OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google Are Teaming Up to Stop Chinese Labs From Copying Their AI

Bloomberg · Japan Times

Three of the biggest rivals in AI — OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — have started sharing intelligence with each other to catch Chinese companies that are copying their models. They’re doing it through the Frontier Model Forum, an industry group they co-founded with Microsoft back in 2023.

The technique they’re trying to stop is called “adversarial distillation” — essentially, someone uses a powerful AI model like ChatGPT or Claude millions of times, feeds the responses into their own system, and trains a cheaper copycat that behaves almost identically. Anthropic previously accused three Chinese labs — DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax — of doing exactly this, claiming they ran over 16 million queries through 24,000 fake accounts. The big concern isn’t just business competition: when someone clones an AI model this way, they typically strip out the safety features that prevent it from being used for harmful purposes.

What does it mean for me? When rivals like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google set aside their competition to work together, it tells you the problem is serious. Chinese labs are allegedly building copycat AI tools by extracting knowledge from American ones — and those copies often don’t include the safety guardrails. If you use any AI tool, the good news is that the companies behind them are actively trying to prevent their technology from being stolen and misused. If you use APIs from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google, expect stricter rate limiting, usage monitoring, and terms of service enforcement. The anti-distillation measures will likely include fingerprinting techniques in model outputs and behavioral analysis of API usage patterns. If you’re building with these APIs at scale, make sure your usage patterns don’t look like distillation attacks, or you may get flagged. On the flip side, if you’re evaluating open-source alternatives, DeepSeek V4 is the model to watch. This is a real-world lesson in intellectual property in the digital age. The idea that you can “copy” an AI by asking it enough questions is counterintuitive and fascinating. If you teach computer science, economics, or even ethics, this is great material. Watch how effective the anti-distillation measures actually are — if they fail, the pricing power of U.S. AI companies is weaker than current valuations assume. Adversarial distillation sits in a murky legal area. Is it a terms of service violation? Copyright infringement? Trade secret theft? The answer depends on jurisdiction, and enforcement across borders is extremely difficult. If you advise tech clients, this collaboration through the Frontier Model Forum could become a model for industry-wide IP protection strategies. It also raises questions about whether API terms of service can legally prohibit certain types of downstream use. This story matters for you because it affects the AI tools you might be paying for. If Chinese labs can successfully clone the best AI models and offer them cheaply (or free), you’ll eventually have access to cheaper alternatives. But those cheaper models may lack safety features that matter when you’re using AI for customer-facing applications. For now, stick with established vendors whose models have proper safety testing. The AI tools you use for content creation, ad copy, and market research are built on models that cost billions to develop. If those models get cloned and offered cheaply, the cost of AI-powered marketing tools could drop significantly. But cheap doesn’t always mean safe — a cloned model without proper guardrails could generate content that gets your brand in trouble. Vet your AI tools carefully. This is a fascinating intersection of technology, geopolitics, and intellectual property law. How do you “steal” an AI model that you can’t physically take? By asking it millions of questions and using the answers to train your own. It’s like studying someone’s teaching style so thoroughly that you can replicate it. Anthropic Just Made Its First Acquisition — a Drug Discovery Startup for $400 Million

TechCrunch · BioSpace · The Information

Anthropic — the company behind Claude — just spent $400 million to acquire Coefficient Bio, a startup that uses AI to speed up drug discovery. It’s Anthropic’s first-ever acquisition, and it signals a major bet that AI can do more than write code and answer questions — it can help discover new medicines.

Coefficient Bio was founded just eight months ago by two researchers from Genentech who specialize in building AI models specifically designed for biology, not general-purpose AI models repurposed for science. That distinction matters: Anthropic didn’t just buy a team that knows how to prompt Claude with biology questions. They bought people who build the kind of AI that understands proteins, drug interactions, and molecular structures from the ground up. The team will join Anthropic’s life sciences group and integrate their work into Claude for Life Sciences, a tool launched last October for scientific researchers.

The deal comes amid a gold rush in AI-powered drug discovery. Eli Lilly signed a $2.75 billion deal with AI drug company Insilico Medicine in March, and Google DeepMind’s Isomorphic Labs is preparing human clinical trials. The bet across the industry is that AI can dramatically shorten the decade-plus timeline it usually takes to bring a new drug to market.

OpenAI Is Reshuffling Its Leadership After Two Executives Step Away for Health Reasons

TechCrunch · CNBC · Bloomberg

OpenAI announced a wave of leadership changes this week, triggered in part by two executives needing to step away for medical reasons. Fidji Simo, who left her role as CEO of Instacart to lead OpenAI’s AGI division, is taking medical leave after a flare-up of POTS — a condition that causes dizziness and fatigue. Chief marketing officer Kate Rouch is also stepping back to continue her cancer recovery.

To fill the gaps, OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman is taking over product responsibilities during Simo’s absence. COO Brad Lightcap is moving to a new “special projects” role focused on high-stakes deals and partnerships, reporting directly to Sam Altman. Denise Dresser, the former CEO of Slack who recently joined as chief revenue officer, is picking up Lightcap’s commercial duties. Former Meta CMO Gary Briggs is stepping in on an interim basis for Rouch.

Three executive changes in one week at the world’s most valuable private company is notable, but OpenAI appears to have planned the transitions carefully. The company is racing to prepare for a potential IPO later this year while managing $2 billion a month in revenue and over 900 million weekly users.

Foxconn Posts Record Revenue as AI Server Demand Explodes

Foxconn — the company best known for manufacturing iPhones — just reported a 29.7% jump in first-quarter revenue to $66.6 billion, driven largely by surging demand for AI servers. March alone was the company’s best month ever, with revenue up 45.6% year-over-year. Foxconn gave 2026 its highest internal growth rating ever — the first time it’s used the “strong growth” designation.

Anthropic Launches a Political Action Committee to Shape AI Regulation

Anthropic officially registered “AnthroPAC” with the Federal Election Commission, making it one of the first major AI companies to directly enter electoral politics. The PAC will be funded by voluntary employee contributions capped at $5,000 per person, with a bipartisan board overseeing donations to lawmakers active on AI policy. The AI industry has already poured roughly $185 million into the 2026 midterm races. Anthropic says it wants to ensure AI regulation is “informed by expertise and a commitment to safety” — though critics see it as another tech company buying political influence.

TechCrunch · CoinTribune

Utah Expands Its First-in-the-Nation AI Prescription Program to Psychiatric Medications

Utah — the only state that allows AI to autonomously renew drug prescriptions — just approved a second pilot program, this time for psychiatric medications. The new pilot lets an AI system renew 15 lower-risk psychiatric drugs that a patient is already taking, though it can’t write new prescriptions, change doses, or handle controlled substances. The original program, launched in January, covers 190 common chronic-condition medications like diabetes and blood pressure drugs. It’s the furthest any state has gone in letting AI make direct decisions in patient care.
