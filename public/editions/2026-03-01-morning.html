Trump Blacklists Anthropic After It Refused to Let the Pentagon Use AI for Surveillance and Autonomous Weapons

Sources: NPR · NBC News · Washington Post · CNBC

The Trump administration just did something extraordinary: it banned an American AI company from doing any business with the U.S. government — not because of fraud or espionage, but because the company drew a line on how its technology could be used. Anthropic, the company behind the AI assistant Claude, has been blacklisted from all federal work after it refused to give the Pentagon unrestricted access to its AI models.

Here's how it went down. Anthropic had a $200 million contract with the Pentagon, signed last July. As part of that deal, Anthropic insisted on two conditions: its AI could not be used for mass surveillance of American citizens, and it could not be used to autonomously kill people without a human making the final decision. The Pentagon wanted those restrictions removed. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth gave Anthropic a deadline — 5:01 p.m. on Friday — to agree. Anthropic didn't budge.

"We will not build technology designed for mass surveillance of Americans or autonomous weapons that kill without human oversight." — Anthropic, in its statement after the ban

What happened next was remarkable. Within hours, OpenAI — Anthropic's biggest rival — announced that it had struck its own deal with the Pentagon to deploy its AI on the department's classified networks. But here's the twist: OpenAI said it insisted on the exact same restrictions Anthropic did. CEO Sam Altman posted on X that "two of our most important safety principles are prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance and human responsibility for the use of force." OpenAI's statement said the Pentagon agreed to put those principles into the contract.

Then the employees got involved. Over 450 workers at Google and OpenAI signed an open letter titled "We Will Not Be Divided," calling on their companies to stand with Anthropic. The letter urged the leadership of both companies to "put aside their differences and stand together to continue to refuse the Department of War's current demands." Almost 400 of the signatures came from Google employees, with the rest from OpenAI.

The bottom line: an AI company just lost all its government business because it wouldn't let the military use its technology without guardrails. Its biggest competitor stepped in — but publicly insisted on the same guardrails. And hundreds of rank-and-file employees across the industry rallied behind the principle. Whether this holds is another question, but it's the most significant moment so far in the debate over how powerful AI should be used by governments.

AI Is Gobbling Up the World's Memory Chips — and Your Next Phone Is Paying the Price

CNN · CNBC · IEEE Spectrum

Here's something that sounds abstract but hits your wallet directly: the AI boom is causing a global shortage of memory chips — the tiny components that let your phone, laptop, and tablet run smoothly. The problem is simple supply and demand. Companies like Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon are buying enormous quantities of memory chips to power their AI data centers, and the three companies that make most of the world's memory (Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron) can only produce so much. They're prioritizing the high-margin AI chips, which means fewer chips for consumer devices.

The result: memory chip prices have surged 80 to 90 percent this quarter alone, and they're expected to rise another 40% in the months ahead. That's getting passed on to you. The average price of a smartphone is expected to hit an all-time high of $523 this year — a 14% jump. Phones that used to cost under $100 may not exist anymore. Laptops, tablets, and even cars with digital systems are all getting more expensive too. And smartphone sales are expected to drop nearly 13% this year to their lowest level in over a decade.

In plain English: the AI companies are buying so many chips that there aren't enough left for your devices, so your devices cost more. It's the first time the AI boom has directly reached into ordinary people's pockets.

Anthropic Quietly Dropped the Safety Promise That Was Supposedly the Whole Point of the Company

TIME · CNN · Bloomberg

While the world was focused on Anthropic's standoff with the Pentagon, the company quietly made another big move — one that might matter even more in the long run. Anthropic removed the central promise from its safety policy: the pledge that it would never release an AI model unless it could guarantee in advance that its safety protections were adequate.

That promise was the whole reason Anthropic existed. The company was founded by former OpenAI leaders who left specifically because they thought OpenAI wasn't taking safety seriously enough. In 2023, Anthropic published its "Responsible Scaling Policy" — a set of rules that said, essentially, "we will slow down or stop building more powerful AI if we can't prove it's safe first." It was the most concrete safety commitment any major AI company had made.

Now that commitment is gone. The updated policy says Anthropic won't pause development if it believes a competitor would release a similar model without safety measures. In plain English: "We'll keep building even if we're not sure it's safe, as long as someone else would build it anyway." The company's reasoning is that if it stops while competitors keep going, the world ends up with powerful AI that has no safety measures at all — which they argue is worse.

The timing is hard to ignore. This change came the same week Anthropic was fighting the Pentagon over its safety principles. Whether you see it as a pragmatic compromise or a broken promise depends on how much faith you put in the original commitment. Either way, the safety company just got a lot less different from everyone else.

What does it mean for me? The AI company that marketed itself as the "safe" option just removed its biggest safety promise. That doesn't mean AI tools are suddenly unsafe — but it does mean that no major AI company is now willing to slow down, even if safety concerns arise. Some of those features will still arrive in March, but the full rollout is being staggered — with some capabilities pushed to a May update, and others potentially delayed until iOS 27 in September.
