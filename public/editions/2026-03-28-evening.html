Anthropic Accidentally Leaked Its Secret Weapon — and It’s Scaring Wall Street

Sources: Fortune · Fortune · Futurism · CNBC · The Decoder

This week, Anthropic — the company behind Claude — accidentally published nearly 3,000 internal documents on an unsecured, publicly searchable server. Among the leaked files: a draft blog post announcing a new AI model called “Claude Mythos” that Anthropic itself describes as posing “unprecedented cybersecurity risks.”

Here’s what the documents revealed. Mythos is part of a new tier Anthropic is calling “Capybara,” and it dramatically outperforms every previous Claude model on tests of coding, academic reasoning, and — most notably — cybersecurity. The model is “currently far ahead of any other AI model in cyber capabilities,” according to the leaked blog post, and Anthropic warns it “presages an upcoming wave of models that can exploit vulnerabilities in ways that far outpace the efforts of defenders.”

In plain English: Anthropic built an AI that’s extremely good at finding and exploiting security holes in software. That’s useful if you’re trying to protect systems — but terrifying if the wrong people get access to it.

“Compared to our previous best model, Claude Opus 4.6, Capybara gets dramatically higher scores on tests of software coding, academic reasoning, and cybersecurity, among others.” — Anthropic leaked draft blog post, via Fortune

Wall Street reacted swiftly. The S&P 500 fell 1.74% on the day the cybersecurity details dropped — the biggest single-day decline in weeks. Cybersecurity stocks were hit particularly hard, with investors worried that an AI this powerful at finding vulnerabilities could upend the entire cybersecurity industry. Software stocks broadly sold off as traders processed the implications of an AI that can find flaws faster than humans can patch them.

The irony wasn’t lost on anyone: the company warning about “unprecedented cybersecurity risks” revealed the model through its own security mistake — a misconfigured content management system. Security researchers Roy Paz of LayerX Security and Alexandre Pauwels of the University of Cambridge discovered the exposed data. Anthropic confirmed the model’s existence rather than denying it, suggesting a public release is coming soon. The company says it’s testing Mythos with a small group of early customers, focusing initially on defensive cybersecurity uses.

What does it mean for me? An AI that’s great at finding security holes is a double-edged sword. If the good guys get it first, your accounts and data get more secure. If attackers figure out how to use models like this, the software you rely on every day becomes more vulnerable. Anthropic says they’re starting with defensive uses, but this is the beginning of an AI-powered arms race in cybersecurity — and the outcome affects everyone who uses the internet. This directly affects your threat model. If Mythos can find exploits faster than human security researchers, then AI-assisted vulnerability scanning is about to become table stakes. Start evaluating AI-powered security tools now, because your adversaries will be using them soon. The flip side: if Anthropic opens this up for defensive use, you may soon have an AI that can audit your codebase for vulnerabilities in minutes instead of weeks. A company accidentally leaked its own secrets while warning about cybersecurity risks — the irony writes itself. Apple Is Quietly Building the Siri That Should Have Existed Years Ago

PYMNTS · MacDailyNews · Business Standard

Bloomberg reported this week that Apple is developing a completely redesigned Siri, codenamed “Project Campos,” that transforms it from a basic voice assistant into a genuine AI agent. The new Siri will be a standalone app on your iPhone with a chat interface, conversation history you can search, and the ability to upload images or files for analysis — basically everything ChatGPT and Claude can do, but built into your phone.

The bigger deal is what Siri will be able to do under the hood. Apple is designing it to work across all your apps — reading your messages, notes, and emails to answer questions with personal context, completing tasks within third-party apps, and acting as what Apple is calling a system-wide AI “agent” that understands what’s on your screen. It will support both voice and text at the same time.

The unveiling is expected at Apple’s developer conference on June 8, as part of iOS 27 and macOS 27. Apple is partnering with Google to use its Gemini model, running on Apple’s Private Cloud Compute infrastructure to maintain the company’s privacy standards.

On the Radar

Meta Built an AI That Simulates How Your Brain Works

Meta released TRIBE v2, an open-source AI model trained on over 1,000 hours of brain scan data from 700+ people. It can predict how your neurons respond to anything you see, hear, or read — and its predictions are more accurate than most real brain scans, which get muddied by heartbeats, movement, and noise. Meta says it could do for brain research what AlphaFold did for protein science: compress months of lab work into seconds of computation. The model, code, and an interactive demo are all free to use.
