Altman Admits His Pentagon Deal Was "Sloppy" — Then Rewrites It Overnight

Sources: CNBC · Fortune · Axios · Gizmodo

If you've been following this story: on Friday, the Trump administration banned Anthropic's AI from all government use after Anthropic refused to give the military unrestricted access to its technology. Hours later, OpenAI swooped in with its own Pentagon deal, claiming it had drawn the same ethical lines. Critics spent the weekend arguing that it hadn't — that OpenAI's contract language was vague enough to quietly allow exactly what Anthropic refused. A Bloomberg opinion piece said Altman had "leaped over the ethical red lines" his competitor refused to cross.

Overnight, Sam Altman conceded. In an internal memo that leaked to social media, he called the original deal "opportunistic and sloppy" — his words — and said the company "shouldn't have rushed" to get it out. He acknowledged it "looked" bad. He then went further: OpenAI and the Pentagon amended the contract to add explicit, written language stating that the AI system "shall not be intentionally used for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons and nationals," citing the Fourth Amendment and the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act by name.

"We were genuinely trying to de-escalate things and avoid a much worse outcome, but I think it just looked opportunistic and sloppy." — Sam Altman, in an internal memo shared on X

Altman also said he told the Pentagon in his conversations over the weekend that Anthropic should not be labeled a national security supply chain risk — the designation that got Anthropic banned in the first place — and that he hopes the government offers Anthropic the same terms OpenAI agreed to. That's a striking statement: the company that just signed the deal is publicly lobbying for its banned competitor to be let back in.

But here's the complication: the boycott isn't stopping. The QuitGPT movement isn't primarily about the exact contract language — it's about whether OpenAI signed any deal at all. The amended wording may satisfy lawyers, but many of the people who canceled their subscriptions weren't reading the contract. They were reacting to the headline. And protests were still going ahead Tuesday morning at OpenAI's San Francisco office. Adding anti-surveillance language to a government deal doesn't un-sign it.

The question now is whether Altman's apology — and the amendment — is enough to stop the bleeding. Claude is still at or near the top of the App Store. Anthropic is still breaking sign-up records. And the workers inside Google and OpenAI are signing letters demanding their own companies draw the same lines Anthropic drew. The story that started Friday isn't close to over.

Jack Dorsey Just Fired Half of Block — and Said Your Company Will Do the Same Within a Year

TechCrunch · CNN · Fortune · Bloomberg

Jack Dorsey — the co-founder of Twitter and the CEO of Block, the payments company behind Square and Cash App — cut nearly 4,000 jobs last week. That's almost half the company's entire workforce, dropping from over 10,000 employees to just under 6,000. And unlike most corporate layoffs, which come with carefully worded statements about "organizational restructuring," Dorsey was unusually blunt about the reason: AI.

In a post on X, Dorsey wrote that the cuts weren't happening because the business was struggling — in fact, he said it wasn't. He said AI tools had fundamentally changed "what it means to build and run a company," and that Block had reached a point where it could do more with fewer people. Then he went further: "Within the next year, I believe the majority of companies will reach the same conclusion and make similar structural changes. I'd rather get there honestly and on our own terms than be forced into it reactively." In other words: this isn't a Block story. This is a preview of what he thinks every company is about to do.

Block's stock surged 20% on the news, which tells you a lot about how Wall Street is thinking about AI-driven headcount reduction right now. But critics pushed back. Bloomberg's analysis noted that Block had 3,835 employees at the end of 2019 — meaning the layoffs were essentially reversing the pandemic-era hiring surge that nearly tripled the company's size. Some analysts called it "AI-washing" — using AI as a convenient explanation for cutting back pandemic overhiring that was always going to unwind. CNN reported that similar patterns are playing out at Amazon, Meta, and Salesforce. The real question: where does the pandemic overhiring story end and the genuine AI displacement story begin? Nobody agrees yet. But Dorsey's "your company is next" prediction is one executives across every industry will be thinking about this week.

CNBC · TechCrunch · Axios

A parallel letter signed by roughly 100 OpenAI employees makes similar demands of its own leadership.

The letters were sparked by the Pentagon's decision to ban Anthropic's AI tools from government use after Anthropic refused to remove those exact limits. The employees aren't just supportive of Anthropic — they're drawing the same line for their own employers. One public letter, titled "We Will Not Be Divided," grew from a few hundred signatures on Friday to nearly 900 by Monday, including employees from both companies. DeepSeek V4 Is About to Drop — A Trillion-Parameter AI Trained Without Nvidia Chips

China's DeepSeek lab is expected to release V4 any day now — likely before China's annual parliamentary meetings begin tomorrow, March 4. V4 is a genuinely significant leap: it's a "multimodal" model, meaning it can understand and generate text, images, and video — not just text like most previous models. It reportedly has a trillion total parameters (roughly, the "knobs" the model uses to make decisions) while only actively using about 32 billion of them at a time, which makes it faster and cheaper to run. It was built and optimized for Huawei and Cambricon chips rather than Nvidia's GPUs, which is a direct response to U.S. export controls. If V4 benchmarks anywhere near GPT-4o or Gemini 1.5 Pro, it will rattle markets and reshape AI pricing almost immediately.
