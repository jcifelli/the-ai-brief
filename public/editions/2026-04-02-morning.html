Anthropic Leaked Its Own Source Code — Then Accidentally Took Down 8,100 GitHub Projects Trying to Fix It

TechCrunch · Fortune · Axios

Anthropic — the AI safety company behind Claude — accidentally published 500,000 lines of its own source code for Claude Code, its popular coding assistant. A debugging file that should have stayed internal was mistakenly bundled into a routine software update and pushed to npm, the public registry where developers download software packages. Within hours, the code had spread across GitHub.

Then the cleanup got worse. Anthropic filed copyright takedown notices (called DMCA notices) asking GitHub to remove the leaked code. But because of how GitHub’s fork system works — when one project is copied, all copies are linked together — the takedown notice accidentally disabled 8,100 repositories, including many that had nothing to do with the leaked code. Developers woke up to find their own projects offline.

Boris Cherny, Anthropic’s head of Claude Code, called the mass takedown “accidental” and retracted most of the notices, limiting the final takedown to one repository and 96 forks that actually contained the leaked code. Anthropic said no customer data was exposed and called it a “packaging issue caused by human error, not a security breach.” But the damage to its reputation as the “safety-first” AI company is real — this is the second time in days it has accidentally exposed internal information, after a separate incident revealed details about an upcoming AI model codenamed Mythos.

Salesforce Just Turned Slack Into an AI Work Assistant — With 30 New Features

TechCrunch · VentureBeat · SiliconANGLE

Salesforce just announced its most ambitious update to Slack since it bought the company five years ago. The update adds 30 new AI-powered features that transform Slackbot — the little helper that used to just set reminders — into a full AI work assistant.

The headline feature is “reusable AI skills” — you can teach Slackbot to do specific tasks (like formatting a weekly report or summarizing a client call), and once set up, anyone on your team can reuse that skill with one click. Slackbot can now transcribe and summarize meetings, so if you miss a call, you can ask it what happened. It can also pop out of the Slack window and sit on your desktop, taking notes and summarizing video calls in real time.

Perhaps the most technically significant change: Slackbot now works with something called the Model Context Protocol (MCP), which means it can connect to outside tools and services — pulling data from your CRM, updating your project tracker, or kicking off automated workflows without you ever leaving Slack. Starting this summer, every new Salesforce customer will get Slack automatically set up with AI features turned on from day one.

A Congressman Is Asking Whether the Anthropic Leak Is a National Security Risk

Rep. Josh Gottheimer (D-NJ), a leading voice on AI and cybersecurity in Congress, sent a letter to Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei demanding an explanation for the source code leak and asking why the company has rolled back certain internal safety protocols. Gottheimer pointed to a previous incident where a Chinese government-linked hacking group targeted Claude, and warned that the leaked code — combined with Anthropic’s upcoming Mythos model — could enable more sophisticated cyberattacks. It’s the first sign that the leak could have political consequences beyond just bad press.

Google Launches Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite — Faster and Dramatically Cheaper

Google released Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite, a stripped-down version of its AI model designed for speed and cost. It runs 2.5 times faster than the previous version and costs just $0.25 per million input tokens (a token is roughly a word — so processing a million words costs a quarter). It’s part of a broader industry shift toward making AI affordable enough for everyday applications like customer support chatbots, automated email responses, and real-time translation — tasks where speed matters more than raw intelligence.
