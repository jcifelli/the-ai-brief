The Two Most Powerful People in AI Stood Side by Side. They Couldn't Even Hold Hands.

Sources: CNBC · TechCrunch · Fortune

At the India AI Impact Summit in New Delhi today, Prime Minister Narendra Modi gathered 13 tech leaders onstage for a show of solidarity. He clasped hands with Google CEO Sundar Pichai on his right and OpenAI CEO Sam Altman on his left, then beckoned everyone to join hands and raise them — like actors taking a bow at the end of a play.

Everyone obliged. Except for two people standing right next to each other: Sam Altman and Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, the company behind the AI model Claude. Instead of joining hands, the two men raised clenched fists into the air without making eye contact. The photo went viral within minutes.

"I didn't know what was happening. When Modi grabbed my hand and put it up, I just wasn't sure what we were supposed to be doing." — Sam Altman to Indian media outlet Moneycontrol

The awkwardness isn't random. Amodei used to be OpenAI's Vice President of Research. He left in 2021 over disagreements with Altman about how fast to push AI development and co-founded Anthropic as a more safety-focused alternative. The two companies have since become the fiercest rivals in AI — a split that boiled over last month when Anthropic ran a Super Bowl ad mocking OpenAI's decision to introduce ads in ChatGPT. Altman fired back publicly, calling Anthropic "dishonest" and "authoritarian."

The rivalry extends well beyond personal tensions. OpenAI is assembling a $100 billion funding round and targeting $30 billion in revenue for 2026. Anthropic just closed a $30 billion round at a $380 billion valuation. Both companies are reportedly preparing IPOs as early as this summer — and both are backed by Sequoia Capital, which is hedging its bet on which company will dominate the next era of computing.

But at the summit itself, the substance ran deeper than the photo op. Amodei used his time onstage to warn about the "serious risks" of AI — including autonomous behavior and mass economic displacement. Altman countered that the industry's definition of AI safety should include "societal resilience" — a broader, more permissive framing. The two visions couldn't be more different: one is sounding the alarm, the other is saying we'll adapt.

Alibaba Releases a Free AI Model It Claims Beats the Best From America

CNBC · WinBuzzer

Chinese tech giant Alibaba released Qwen 3.5, a new AI model with 397 billion parameters — the number that roughly corresponds to a model's brainpower. The clever part: it uses a design called "mixture of experts" that only activates 17 billion parameters at a time, making it dramatically faster and cheaper to run. Alibaba published benchmark tests claiming Qwen 3.5 outperforms OpenAI's GPT-5.2, Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.5, and Google's Gemini 3 Pro, though those results are self-reported and haven't been independently verified.

The model is free to download and use, supports 201 languages, and can understand text, images, audio, and video simultaneously. It can also act on its own — navigating apps on your phone or computer to complete tasks without you clicking anything. Alibaba says it costs 60% less to run than its predecessor. A coding-specific version, Qwen3.5-Coder, is designed specifically for writing and debugging software.

Microsoft Pledges $50 Billion to Close the AI Gap Between Rich and Poor Countries

Microsoft · CNN

At the India AI Impact Summit, Microsoft announced it's on track to invest $50 billion by the end of this decade to expand AI access across the "Global South" — a term for developing countries across Africa, Latin America, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. The problem Microsoft is trying to solve: people in wealthier countries use AI about twice as much as people in developing ones, and that gap is widening.

The investment includes building data centers (the physical facilities where AI actually runs), training 20 million people in AI skills by 2028, and launching a food security initiative in Kenya that uses AI to analyze satellite images of farmland. Microsoft has already invested $8 billion in data centers across India, Mexico, Africa, and Southeast Asia in the last year alone. Separately, Google committed $15 billion and Amazon pledged $35 billion for AI infrastructure in India at the same summit.

SpaceX Swallows Elon Musk's AI Company in the Largest Merger Ever

CNBC · TechCrunch

SpaceX has officially acquired xAI — Elon Musk's artificial intelligence startup behind the Grok chatbot — in a deal valuing the combined company at $1.25 trillion. That makes it the largest corporate merger in history. SpaceX issued $250 billion in new shares to xAI's shareholders to close the transaction.

Musk's stated goal is to build data centers in space — orbiting, solar-powered computing facilities that he argues will be the cheapest way to run AI within two to three years. SpaceX has asked the FCC for permission to launch a constellation of one million satellites to support the idea. The merger also brings the Grok chatbot, the X social media platform, and Starlink's satellite internet under a single corporate umbrella — ahead of a planned IPO reportedly targeted for mid-June. Critics note that xAI is currently burning about $1 billion per month, and that space-based computing depends on launch costs dropping far beyond what anyone has achieved.
