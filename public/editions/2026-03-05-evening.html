The Pentagon Just Treated Anthropic the Way It Treats Huawei. That’s Never Happened to an American Company Before.

Sources: TechCrunch · NBC News · CNBC · CNN

Here’s what happened: The Department of Defense officially notified Anthropic today that it has been designated a “supply-chain risk to national security.” In plain English, the Pentagon is saying Anthropic’s AI products are a threat to the military’s supply chain — and it’s banning the Department of Defense and its contractors from using them. President Trump directed federal agencies to “immediately cease” all use of Anthropic’s technology, with a six-month phaseout period.

Why this matters: until today, this designation had only ever been used against companies from adversary nations — most famously Huawei, the Chinese telecom giant. It has never been applied to an American company. Anthropic is a San Francisco-based AI startup that makes Claude, one of the most widely used AI assistants in the world.

“It is the first time a U.S. company has received such a designation, which until now was reserved for firms from adversary countries.” — CNBC

The backstory: this dispute has been building for weeks. The Pentagon wanted full, unrestricted access to Anthropic’s AI models for any lawful military purpose. Anthropic pushed back, insisting on guardrails — specifically, assurances that its technology wouldn’t be used for fully autonomous weapons (weapons that can select and engage targets without a human in the loop) or for mass surveillance of American citizens. The two sides couldn’t reach an agreement, and the relationship deteriorated from there.

The immediate fallout has been dramatic. Government agencies including the State Department, Treasury, and HHS are replacing Anthropic’s products with OpenAI’s GPT-4.1 and Google’s Gemini. Defense contractors like Lockheed Martin are cutting ties. But here’s the twist: even as the ban took effect, Bloomberg reported that Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has reopened negotiations with the Pentagon, suggesting a deal could still be reached.

Meanwhile, the public has picked a side. Anthropic’s Claude app has surged to the top of Apple’s App Store download charts, driven by users who support the company’s stance. And nearly 900 employees across Google, OpenAI, and other AI companies have signed an open letter calling for clearer limits on how their employers work with the military.

This story is far from over. The question at the center of it — should AI companies be able to set conditions on how the military uses their technology, or does the government get final say? — is one that will shape the AI industry for years to come.

Yahoo Finance · Techi · U.S. News

The backstory makes this decision understandable: even though the U.S. government issued Nvidia a license last week to ship “small amounts” of H200 chips to China, not a single one has actually been sold. Beijing never formally approved the imports on its end. So Nvidia had chips it was allowed to sell but no buyers — and meanwhile, demand for its newest hardware from Western customers is through the roof. Rather than keep building chips that are sitting in a warehouse, Nvidia chose to use that factory time for products people are actually buying.

This is a major signal. Nvidia isn’t just pausing China sales — it’s shutting down the production line entirely. That suggests the company doesn’t expect meaningful business with China anytime soon, regardless of what the export rules allow.

Bigger orders — up to 100,000 chips — would require government-to-government assurances and security promises from the buyer. And the largest shipments (hundreds of thousands of chips, the kind needed to train the most powerful AI models) would only go to U.S. allies whose governments agree to site visits, security conditions, and investments in American AI infrastructure.
